1.3 Commenting Code

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Define all of our time variables
delta_t = 0.1
t = 0.0
t_refractory = 3    # Refractory period after a spike has occurred
t_rest = -1         # The current rest time

# Define variables for the integrate and fire equation
voltage = 0.0
resistance = 5
tau = 10

# Define variables for the spike and threshold
threshold = 4
spike = 2
spiking = False     # Whether or not we are currently spiking

# Initialization of our arrays
voltages = [voltage]
times = [t]
current = []

# Setup some default currents. We append some zeroes to the beginning to offset the graph
for y in range(0, 500):
    if y > 50:
        current.append(1)
    else:
        current.append(0)

# Loop through the function
for z in range(1, 500):
    # If we are not in a resting time (after a spike), calculate the next voltage,
    # otherwise use the voltage from the previous step (0)
    if t > t_rest:
        voltage = voltages[z-1] + (((resistance * current[z-1]) - voltages[z-1]) / tau) * delta_t
    else:
        voltage = voltages[z-1]

    # If the voltage is above our threshold, check if we are already spiking. If we are,
    # set the voltage back to zero, say that we are no longer spiking, and set a resting period
    # during which the voltage will not change. Otherwise, if we are not already spiking but we
    # are above the threshold, and the spike voltage, then indicate we are spiking
    if voltage > threshold:
        if spiking:
            voltage = 0
            spiking = False
            t_rest = t + t_refractory
        else:
            voltage += spike
            spiking = True

    # Append the new voltage to the array
    voltages.append(voltage)

    # Calculate the new time and append it to our time array
    t = times[z-1] + delta_t
    times.append(t)

# Plot the results
plt.plot(times, voltages)
plt.axis([0, t, -1, 7])
plt.xlabel('Time')
plt.ylabel('Voltage')
plt.show()

2 Who were Hodgkin and Huxley

2.1 Alan Lloyd Hodgkin

  • (1914 - 1998)
  • 1939 begins collaboration with Huxley (most of the war worked on radar)
  • Work was published in the early 50's and received Nobel 1963
  • Bio Hodgkin Father died during WWI. At 18 he entered Trinity College, Cambridge. He had family that were historians and he wavered between history and science. Began his studies of electrophysiology with the shore crab. Learned to dissect squid axons at Woods Hole. Brief anecdotal digression here about Woods Hole.

2.2 Andrew F. Huxley

  • 1917
  • Attended Westminster School and then Trinity College, Cambridge.
  • Went to Cambridge with a concentration in Physical Sciences
  • Rules required him to take a natural science course and he chose physiology.
  • Thought about changing to medicine.
  • When war began, switched from medical to operational research in gunnery.
  • Bio Huxley Father was Thomas Huxley, children of his first marriage included Julian Huxley and Aldous Huxley. Had started in classics before converting to science.
  • Important Points Emphasize Emphasize the cross disciplinary nature of both of their training. Also, how they did not just stay camped out at Cambridge, but traveled to learn new skills and techniques. Note also that the new technological developments played a key role in their progress and approach. Neither was trained as a neurophysiologist. They learned what they needed to learn.

3.1.2 Further background

Calculators are Human Too. It took them only a month, in a laboratory damaged by air raids in 1941 but subsequently repaired, to record all the data that they used for their five key papers. Took them two years to settle on the equations and parameters that they were going to use. They generated their fits from the resting state, and then "applied" a stimulus to see if it generated an action potential.

H and H had hoped to used the EDSAC, one of the first electronic computers in England, but it was undergoing major modifications, so they solved the equations numerically with a mechanical Brunsviga desk calculator. This meant that each calculation took a matter of days. For action potentials it was a matter of weeks.

Moral: data is critical, but the amount of time spent in planning for its collection and analyzing it usually far exceeds the actual collection time.

Moral: test with a subset of data and using remaining data as a test of the model.

3.1.3 Recall the I and F

The Hodgkin and Huxley Model Recall our integrate and fire model: \[I_{total}(t) = I_{c}(t) + \sum_i I_i (t),\] where the \(i\) refers to all other sources of current.

The $i$'s reflect Na, K, and a "Leak" (or fudge factor).

3.1.4 Now look at the equation

\[\sum_i I(t) = \bar{g}_{Na} m^3 h (V(t) - E_{Na}) + \bar{g}_{K} n^4 (V(t) - E_{K}) + \bar{g}_{L} (V(t) - E_{L})\]

Comments on H&H Note that the equations are explicitly time varying. Question: What is the alternative way of expressing the capacitive current that we have learned?

Answer: $= C\frac{dV}{dt}.$

Using Ohm's law (Question: What is Ohm's Law? Answer: $V=IR.$) we can restate the currents in terms of conductances (inverse of resistance) and voltage. Note that the equations become complex because each of the currents is voltage dependent; the currents change depending on the voltage, because the conductances change based on the voltage.

\(g\) is the conventional abbreviation for conductance. The common way to write the model is by using \(\bar{g}\) 's, which are the conductances at maximum. Question: What do the \(E_{\cdot}\) 's represent? May have to try and lead them to this by having them consider what happens to the sign of each term when the \(E>V(t)\) Answer: Ion dependent reversal potential.

What are the \(m\) 's, \(n\) 's, and \(h\) 's?

  • Hypothesized to represent the probability of channel opening.
  • The actual numbers came from data fitting.
  • Each of the \(m\), \(n\), and \(h\) has its own differential equation.
  • \(\dot{m} = \alpha_m (V)(1 - m) - \beta_m (V) m\)

3.1.5 Why Care?

Predictive Power

When the sodium and potassium channels where finally sequenced how many subunits were they discovered to have? Answer: 4.

Moral: If you can predict something before you know it, then you have a powerful model.

3.1.6 Nuances

Question: What does the dot over the \(m\) mean? Answer: derivative with respect to time.

Each of the \(m\), \(n\), and \(h\) have the identical formula. Just substitute the proper subscripts. The values of the $α$'s and $ β $'s are, again, empirically, determined.

Modeling

  • This is a hard algorithm to code, because everything depends on something else.

How do you know where to start?

  • If you allow \(t \rightarrow \infty \mbox{, then } \frac{dV}{dt}=\)?
  • You assume that it goes to zero; that is, you reach steady state. Then you can solve for some of the constants.
